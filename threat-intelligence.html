What Is Threat Intelligence?

Threat Intelligence is evidence-based knowledge about adversaries, their infrastructure, tools, techniques, and intent.
It is derived from analyzing indicators, campaigns, malware samples, attacker behavior, and real-world incidents.
The primary goal is to support better detection, faster response, and informed decision-making in security operations.

Types of Threat Intelligence

Strategic Intelligence
High-level trends, threat landscape reports, and adversary motivations. Used by leadership and risk teams.
Operational Intelligence
Campaigns, attack timelines, malware families, and actor activity. Used by SOC leads and IR teams.
Tactical Intelligence
Indicators of Compromise (IPs, domains, URLs, hashes). Used directly in detections and blocking.
Technical Intelligence
TTPs, exploits, tools, and malware behavior. Mapped to MITRE ATT&CK.

IOC vs Threat Intelligence

Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) are raw data points such as IPs, domains, URLs, and file hashes.
Threat Intelligence provides context around IOCs ‚Äî who is using them, why, how, and as part of which campaign.
Blocking IOCs without context can lead to false positives, missed attacks, or unnecessary disruption.

When Threat Intelligence Is Used

Enrich IOCs generated from SIEM, EDR, or email security alerts
Identify whether activity is part of a known campaign
Track threat actor infrastructure and malware families
Improve detection rules and proactive threat hunting

SOC Analyst Notes

Threat intelligence should be used for context, not as the sole decision-making factor.
Always correlate TI findings with internal telemetry (SIEM, EDR, firewall logs).
Be cautious of stale IOCs; prioritize recent sightings and campaign activity.
Feed validated intelligence back into detection and hunting workflows.
